The Physics

How It Works

The Ising model is one of the simplest models in physics that produces a phase transition — a sudden, dramatic change in behavior at a specific temperature, much like water freezing into ice.

🧭

Tiny Compass Needles

Each atom is modeled as a tiny magnet that can only point up (+1) or down (-1). Neighbors prefer to align. That is the entire model — one rule, endlessly rich behavior.

🌡️

Temperature = Chaos

Temperature controls how much random jiggling each atom feels. Cold: atoms stay aligned, the material is magnetic. Hot: randomness wins, magnetism vanishes.

✨

The Critical Point

At one exact temperature, the system is poised between order and chaos. Fluctuations appear at every scale — tiny clusters and huge domains coexist. This is where the most interesting physics happens.

📏

Finite-Size Scaling

Real magnets have 1023 atoms. We simulate thousands. By running at multiple sizes and extrapolating, we recover the behavior of an infinite system — and measure critical exponents.

❄️

Kibble-Zurek Mechanism

Cool a magnet through Tc quickly and defects freeze in — patches that chose different alignments. The number of defects follows a universal power law. The same physics governs defects in the early universe.

🧲

Real Materials

By tuning one parameter (coupling strength J) on the right crystal lattice, the simulation reproduces the Curie temperatures of real iron (1043 K) and nickel (631 K).

Key Figures

Phase Transition — The Main Event

These figures show how thermodynamic quantities change as temperature sweeps through the critical point. The sharp peaks and crossings are how physicists pin down the exact transition temperature and measure critical exponents.

Thermodynamic observables Energy, magnetization, heat capacity, and susceptibility for lattice sizes L=8 to L=48. The phase transition appears as features that sharpen with increasing system size.
Binder cumulant crossing All lattice sizes cross at the same temperature — that is Tc. This method pins down the critical temperature to 4.512(4) J/kB.
Scaling collapse When axes are rescaled using critical exponents, data from all lattice sizes falls onto one universal curve. This is the signature of a continuous phase transition.
Peak scaling Log-log plots of peak heights vs system size. The slopes give the critical exponent ratios γ/ν and β/ν.

Three Universality Classes on GPU

The same code, run on an NVIDIA RTX 2060, simulates three fundamentally different types of magnetism. Despite different microscopic rules, each shows the same universal scaling behavior — with its own set of critical exponents.

Ising observables Discrete up/down spins. Sharp transition at Tc = 4.51.

Validation

Five independent tests confirm the simulator gives correct answers before we trust any new results.

Onsager exact solution The gold standard: our 2D Monte Carlo results land right on Onsager's 1944 analytical curve.
Exact enumeration For a tiny 4x4 lattice, we can check every possible configuration. Monte Carlo agrees perfectly.
Known limits At T=0, full order. At T=infinity, full disorder. Both limits reproduced exactly.

Beyond Equilibrium

The simulator also probes dynamic phenomena — what happens when you cool a magnet through the phase transition, or remove random bonds from the crystal.

Kibble-Zurek mechanism Defect density vs cooling rate follows a power law. Our exponent (0.258) matches the theoretical prediction (0.279) to within 7%.
Bond dilution Randomly removing bonds weakens the magnet and lowers Tc — smoothly and predictably, matching theoretical expectations.
Domain coarsening After a quench, magnetic domains grow as t1/2, confirming Allen-Cahn dynamics.
Machine learning A neural network, given raw spin snapshots with no physics knowledge, independently discovers the phase transition temperature.
